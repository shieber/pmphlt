I’ve written in the past about the fairness algorithm “cut and choose” (in particular in the context of solving the gerrymandering problem). Just today, I tripped over an especially early example of its use, in 1 Kings 18:22–24. The prophet Elijah, proponent of Adonai, proposes a contest with the 450 prophets of the god Ba‘al: Whose god can deliver a miracle of the self-lighting burnt offering. They’ll both prepare a burnt offering, but without providing a fire. Whoever’s offering burns, their god wins. But to be fair, Elijah let’s the prophets of Ba‘al pick which of the two bulls they want to use; that way, there can’t be a rigged bull. After much unsportsmanlike taunting of Ba‘al’s prophets by Elijah, and not surprisingly (it’s the Hebrew bible after all), Elijah’s burnt offering is struck by heavenly fire.

Stuff like that apparently used to happen.
